# Business Formation — Setting Up a Legal Entity

## Why Form a Business Entity?

When you sell bots, prompts, or AI services, you are conducting commerce. Without a formal business entity, you are a **sole proprietor by default** — meaning you and your business are legally the same person. This matters because:

- **Personal liability**: If someone sues over your product, they can go after your personal assets (savings, car, home).
- **Tax exposure**: All business income flows directly to your personal tax return.
- **Credibility**: Many marketplaces, payment processors, and clients prefer or require a formal business entity.
- **IP ownership**: Forming an entity lets you assign your intellectual property to the business, separating it from you personally.

## Common Entity Types

### Sole Proprietorship
- **What it is**: You operating as yourself. No filing required.
- **Pros**: Zero setup friction, simple taxes.
- **Cons**: No liability protection. You are personally responsible for everything.
- **Good for**: Testing an idea before committing. Not recommended for ongoing product sales.

### LLC (Limited Liability Company)
- **What it is**: A separate legal entity that provides liability protection. The most popular choice for small creators and indie sellers.
- **Pros**: Personal liability protection, flexible taxation (taxed as sole prop, partnership, or S-Corp), simple operating agreement, credible to clients and platforms.
- **Cons**: Annual state fees and reporting requirements vary by state. Not free.
- **Good for**: Most independent bot/prompt sellers. Strong default choice.

### S-Corporation
- **What it is**: A corporation with a special tax election. Profits flow through to shareholders (you) without corporate tax, but you must pay yourself a "reasonable salary."
- **Pros**: Can reduce self-employment tax at higher income levels.
- **Cons**: More complex. Requires payroll. More restrictions on ownership.
- **Good for**: Creators earning $60,000+ in net profit who want to optimize taxes. Usually comes after forming an LLC first.

### C-Corporation
- **What it is**: A full corporation. Two layers of tax (corporate + personal on dividends).
- **Pros**: Preferred by VCs, can issue multiple stock classes, unlimited shareholders.
- **Cons**: Expensive, complex, double taxation unless structured carefully.
- **Good for**: If you plan to raise outside investment. Not for most indie creators.

## Recommended Path for Most Bot Sellers

1. **Start with an LLC** in your state.
2. Get an **EIN** (Employer Identification Number) from the IRS — free, used like a Social Security number for your business.
3. Open a **separate business bank account** — critical for maintaining liability protection.
4. As revenue grows, talk to a CPA about whether an **S-Corp election** makes sense.

## How to Form an LLC

Every state is different, but the process is generally:

1. **Choose a state** — Most people form in their home state. Delaware and Wyoming are popular for their flexible laws, but for small businesses, your home state is usually simpler and cheaper.
2. **Choose a name** — Search your state's Secretary of State business name database to confirm availability. The name typically must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company."
3. **File Articles of Organization** — The foundational document filed with your state's Secretary of State. Filing fee ranges from $50 (Kentucky) to $500+ (Massachusetts). Average is $100–$150.
4. **Get an EIN** — Free at `IRS.gov/EIN`. Takes 5 minutes online.
5. **Create an Operating Agreement** — Not always legally required, but strongly recommended. Defines ownership, profit splits, and how decisions are made. Templates exist online; an attorney can customize one.
6. **Open a business bank account** — Bring your Articles of Organization and EIN.
7. **Register for state taxes if required** — Some states require sales tax registration if you sell digital goods.

## DBA (Doing Business As)

If your LLC is named "Molina Ventures LLC" but you sell under the brand "Bot Ventures," you may need to file a **DBA** (also called a fictitious business name or trade name) with your state or county. This lets you legally do business and receive payments under your brand name.

## Registered Agent

LLCs are required to have a **registered agent** — a person or company with a physical address in the formation state who can receive legal documents on your behalf. You can serve as your own agent, or hire a registered agent service (typically $50–$150/year).

## Key Ongoing Requirements

- **Annual report / franchise tax**: Most states require a yearly filing and fee to keep your LLC active.
- **Separate finances**: Never mix personal and business money. This "piercing the corporate veil" can eliminate your liability protection.
- **Contracts in the entity's name**: Sign agreements as "Your LLC, by [Your Name], Member" — not as yourself personally.
