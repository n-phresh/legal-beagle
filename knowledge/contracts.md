# Contracts — Protecting Yourself When You Sell

## Why Contracts Matter

When you sell a bot, a prompt, a session, or a service built on AI, you are creating a legal relationship with your buyer. Without a contract, the terms of that relationship are ambiguous — and ambiguity tends to favor whoever has more resources to argue their interpretation.

Contracts let you define:
- What the buyer gets (and what they don't)
- What you will and won't support
- Who owns what after the transaction
- What happens when things go wrong

## Types of Agreements You'll Likely Need

### 1. Terms of Service (ToS) / End User License Agreement (EULA)

The foundational agreement between you and anyone who uses or buys your product.

Key clauses to include:
- **License grant**: What the buyer can do with your bot/prompt (use, modify, resell, sublicense?)
- **Restrictions**: What they cannot do (reverse engineer, re-sell as their own, use for illegal purposes)
- **IP ownership**: You retain all intellectual property rights. The buyer gets a license, not ownership.
- **No warranty / limitation of liability**: Your bot is provided "as is." You are not liable for their business outcomes.
- **Acceptable use policy**: Prohibited uses (spam, abuse, illegal activity)
- **Termination**: When you can revoke access

### 2. Privacy Policy

Required if you collect any personal information (even just an email address for a mailing list). Many states (California, Virginia, Colorado) and countries (EU GDPR) have specific requirements.

### 3. Refund / Cancellation Policy

Digital products are often non-refundable, but you must state this clearly. Vague policies lead to chargebacks.

### 4. Freelance / Client Service Agreement

If you build custom bots or sessions for clients (not just selling off-the-shelf products), you need a project agreement that defines:
- Scope of work
- Deliverables and timeline
- Payment terms
- Who owns the final product (work-for-hire vs. license)
- Revision limits
- Confidentiality / NDA clauses

### 5. Collaborator / Contributor Agreement

If you work with other people on your bots, define upfront:
- Who owns the IP
- Revenue splits
- What happens if someone leaves

## IP Ownership in Client Work — Critical Clause

This is the most commonly misunderstood area for creators:

- By default under U.S. copyright law, the creator owns the work — not the client.
- If you want a client to own the finished bot, you must have a **written work-for-hire agreement** or an **IP assignment clause**.
- If you want to retain the underlying IP and only license it to the client, the contract must say that explicitly.

**Recommended default for most bot sellers**: You retain ownership of all underlying prompts, methods, and systems. The client receives a license to use the delivered product.

## Where to Get Contract Templates

- **Clerky** and **Stripe Atlas** offer templates for tech businesses
- **HelloSign / Docusign** have basic template libraries
- **SCORE** (free small business mentoring) has free template resources
- **Creative Commons** has licensing templates if you want to open-source your prompts
- A business attorney can draft custom agreements tailored to your product

## Marketplace Platform Contracts

If you sell through a marketplace (e.g., Gumroad, Lemon Squeezy, PromptBase, etc.):
- Read their seller agreement carefully, especially the IP section
- Some platforms claim a license to your content
- Understand their dispute and chargeback policies
- Know what data they collect about your buyers (affects your privacy obligations)

## Disclaimer

Contracts are legally binding documents. Have an attorney review any agreement before relying on it for significant transactions.
