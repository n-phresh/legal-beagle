# Copyright — Protecting Your Creative Work

## What Is Copyright?

Copyright is the automatic legal protection that attaches to original creative works the moment they are fixed in a tangible form. For bot builders, this includes:

- The text of your prompts and system instructions
- The personality, tone, and character descriptions you write
- Your documentation, README files, and training narratives
- Custom knowledge bases you author
- The code you write (scripts, session logic, tool definitions)
- Any written content your bots produce (with caveats — see AI Output section below)

## What Copyright Does NOT Protect

- **Ideas, concepts, or facts** — only the specific expression of them
- **Names, titles, short phrases** — these fall under trademark law
- **Functional elements** of software (those may fall under patent)
- **Purely AI-generated content** (currently a major gray area — see below)

## Is Copyright Automatic?

**Yes — with caveats.**

In the U.S., copyright attaches automatically when you create an original work. You don't need to register or put a © notice. But registration gives you important advantages:

| Without Registration | With Registration |
|---|---|
| You own it | You own it |
| You can sue for infringement | You can sue for infringement |
| You can only recover actual damages | You can recover statutory damages ($750–$150,000 per work) |
| You must prove damages | Attorney's fees may be awarded |
| No public record of your ownership | Public record exists |

**Bottom line**: Registration is not required but strongly recommended before you publish or sell anything.

## How Long Does Copyright Last?

For works created by individuals: **life of the author + 70 years**.

For works made for hire (business-owned): **95 years from publication** or **120 years from creation**, whichever is shorter.

## How to Register

1. Go to the U.S. Copyright Office: `https://copyright.gov`
2. Create an account and use the **eCO** (electronic Copyright Office) system
3. Choose the right work type: Literary Works (for text/prompts), Computer Programs (for code), or other applicable categories
4. Pay the filing fee (as of 2024: $45–$65 for a single work online)
5. Upload a deposit copy of your work
6. Wait for processing (typically 3–7 months)

You can register **multiple works in one registration** as a collection if they were created by the same author and published together.

## The © Symbol

You can use © without registration. Format: `© [Year] [Your Name or Business Name]`

Example: `© 2026 Brandon Molina`

Put this in your documentation, product pages, and anywhere you publish prompts or bots.

## AI Output and Copyright — The Gray Area

This is the most important and unsettled area for bot builders.

**Current U.S. Copyright Office position (as of 2024):**
- Copyright requires human authorship.
- Content generated entirely by AI with no meaningful human creative input is **not copyrightable**.
- Content where a human makes significant creative choices (selects, arranges, edits AI output) **may be** partially copyrightable for those human-selected elements.

**What this means for you:**
- The prompts, instructions, and session designs you write ARE copyrightable (you wrote them).
- The raw output a user generates by running your bot is in a gray zone.
- If you curate, edit, and arrange AI outputs into a product (a knowledge base, a report template, etc.), those editorial choices may be protectable.

**Best practice**: Register the human-authored elements (your prompts, instructions, designs) and clearly document your creative contributions.

## Key Practical Steps for Bot Sellers

1. Put a © notice on everything you publish.
2. Register your core prompt files and documentation with the U.S. Copyright Office.
3. Keep version history (git history counts as evidence of creation timeline).
4. Use clear Terms of Service that define what buyers can and cannot do with your bots.
5. If you publish prompts on marketplaces, check their IP ownership clauses carefully.
